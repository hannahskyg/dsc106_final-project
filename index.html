Did people get richer after COVID!?

US Households' Financial Situation Pre- and Post-COVID Across Different Demographics

By: Hannah Gonzalez, Edwards Tong, Janie Chan, and Junran Yang

Wealth is Growing… or is it?

Between 2019 and 2022, the average American household saw their net worth increase significantly in nominal terms.

Although nominal net worth increased between 2019 and 2022, inflation eroded much of the gain. In real terms (inflation-adjusted), wealth grew far less — suggesting households did not become as wealthy as it appears.

But... Not Everyone Benefits Equally

Breaking down the data by sex reveals disparities. Female-headed households experienced a smaller nominal increase in net worth than male-headed households.

After adjusting for inflation, the gender gap widens. Real net worth grew far more for male-headed households than female-headed households, showing unequal financial resilience across genders.

Age Tells a Different Story

Middle-aged households (45–64) hold significantly more nominal wealth than younger or older households. Younger households (under 35) have far lower net worth.

Inflation-adjusted trends show that younger households lost even more ground. While older and middle-aged groups preserved more real wealth, younger households' real net worth improved only minimally.

Education, the Problem

Households with lower education levels have significantly less nominal net worth.

When adjusted for inflation, all different kinds of educational level had a decrease for networth.

Therefore... Inequality Persists

Despite overall nominal growth, the gains of economic expansion remain uneven across gender, age, and education.

When we adjust for inflation, inequality becomes even more visible. Real wealth growth is concentrated among older, male, and highly educated households — widening disparities across the population.
